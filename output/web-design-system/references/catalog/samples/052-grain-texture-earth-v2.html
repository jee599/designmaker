How we think

Perfectly imperfect

On asymmetry

Centered layouts feel manufactured. We offset things — a heading 3rem left, an image slightly rotated, a card that doesn't quite align with its neighbor. The eye relaxes when things aren't too perfect.

On aging

Most software fights against time. We embrace it. Our interfaces develop character through use — worn paths show where you've been, like creases in a leather wallet.

On restraint

Two colors. One typeface. Paper texture. That's it. Every additional element must justify its existence in a room that's already quiet enough.

"They delivered a site that looked like it had existed for years. Our customers said it felt 'familiar' on day one. I've never gotten that feedback before."

— founder, ceramics studio, 8 months post-launch

Process

How it works

01

Listen

We spend the first week not designing. We read your brand, visit your space (if physical), talk to your customers. The texture comes from reality, not mood boards.

02

Shape

Low-fidelity first. Pen on paper, then browser. We skip Figma — the medium matters. Things made in browsers feel like websites. Things made in Figma feel like pictures of websites.

03

Age

We launch, then revisit after 3 months. What paths did users wear? What feels stiff? We adjust — not redesign. The site grows into itself.

Materials

What we work with

Hand-lettered type

Custom letterforms drawn by hand, then digitized with imperfections intact. No Bezier cleanup — the wobble is the point.

Film photography

All product photography shot on Portra 400. Scanned at home. Grain is part of the palette.

Vanilla CSS

No framework churn. CSS that will work in 10 years the same way it works today. Boring technology, lasting results.

Long-form copy

We write, not bullet-point. Stories take space. If the reader stays, the writing earned it.
